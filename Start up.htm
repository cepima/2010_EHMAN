GASIFIER

Gasification is the conversion of biomass into a combustible gas mixture by the partial oxidation of biomass at high temperatures, typically in the range 800–1100 ºC. The biomass resource can be considered as any type of organic matter, in which the energy of sunlight is stored in chemical bond. Therefore, it has proven to be the most successful design to produce power generation. It is one of the most the suitable technologies for small-scale biomass.

The start-up of a new pilot plant downdraft gasifier was carried out in our installations. This pilot plant is a gasification technology to convert biomass into a fuel suitable for use in a gas engine to generate electricity and heat. The gasification process operates with a system based on a downdraft gasifier carried out to use widely range of biomass. This type of gasifier was developed to convert biomass to low tar gas.

In addition, biomass gasification system consists primarily of a gasifier, a feeding system, an air supply system, a cyclone, a scrubber, heat exchangers, a mesh filter, a gas hopper and a gas engine.

The following image shows a photograph of the gasifier.

The gasification process has four phases:

1.    Biomass pretreatment, where initially a selection of raw material has been made. Then, this raw material is storaged for a posterior usage.

2.    Conversion of biomass in the gasifier by the thermochemical process, including drying, pyrolysis, oxidation and reduction. The syngas is produced in this phase.

3.    Syngas conditioning is carried out to clean it of impurities. After this cleansing, the syngas goes through a heat exchanger system to reduce its temperature, and finally, it goes through the scrubber in order to eliminate condensable material, such as tars.

4.    Energy recovery, by the usage of syngas in a gas based engine in which the chemical energy release by burning the gas.

Our effort is focused on:

·         Use different types of biomass susceptible to use in the pilot plant.

·         Design and operating conditions.

·         Complete plant monitoring and control of the entire process.

·         Improve the system performance by cogeneration.

·         Develop a model using Aspen-Hysis.

 

PILOT PLANT PHOTO-FENTON

Photo-Fenton process is an advanced oxidation process (AOP) leading to the formation of hydroxyl radical, ·OH, which is the second most powerful oxidant present in nature after fluoride. During the reaction, hydrogen peroxide oxidizes ferrous iron (or iron ion II) by oxidation-reduction:

Mixture of ferrous iron and hydrogen peroxide is called Fenton's reagent and is a very good oxidant for many organic compounds.

Ferrous ion photo activation leds to further hydroxyl radicals production by the reaction, especially when wavelength radiation is between 300 and 650nm:

Nowadays, photo-Fenton reaction is an effective method to fight against organic micro-pollutants present in industrial and agricultural effluents and, furthermore, for wastewater treatment, as is our project’s case.

WASTEWATER TREATMENT PLANT SIMULATION

The wastewater treatment (WWT) is the process of removing contaminants from wastewater, due to domestic residences, commercial properties or industrial and agriculture activities. This treatment includes physical, chemical, and biological processes to remove physical, chemical and biological contaminants. Its objective is to produce an environmentally-safe fluid waste stream (recycled water) and a solid waste (sludges) suitable for disposal or reuse, usually as farm fertilizer.

The following scheme represents the wastewater treatment station diagram:

The system is described by 8 processes representing the behavior of growth and decay of the species in the WWT system:

·        Aerobic growth of heterotrophic biomass XBH.

·        Anoxic growth of heterotrophic biomass (denitrification).

·        Aerobic growth of autotrophic biomass XB,A (nitrification).

·        Decay of heterotrophic biomass.

·        Decay of autotrophic biomass.

·        Ammonification of soluble organic nitrogen.

·        Hydrolysis of entrapped organics.

·        Hydrolysis of entrapped organic nitrogen.

It is important to optimize this kind of plants due to:

·        Wastewater treatment plants are emerging.

·        Tighter laws on water quality.

·        High costs associated with construction and operation of the wastewater treatment systems.

A wastewater treatment has been modeled using Matlab and Simulink. It provides an interactive graphical environment and a customizable set of block libraries that let you design, simulate, implement, and test a variety of time-varying systems, including communications, controls, signal processing, video processing, and image processing.

PLASTIC PRODUCTION SIMULATION

The simulation of a plastic industry can be used to determine the amount of products to be treated by wastewater treatment or by Photo-Fenton treatment. Particularly, it has been studied the case of polyethylene terephthalate (PET) production.

Polyethylene terephthalate is one of the most important polymers from a commercial point of view. This polymer is the raw material for the production of synthetic fibres, films, filaments and plastic objects. It is synthesized though dimethyl terephthalate or terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol.

In this process, the main sub-products to be treated are methanol, ethylene glycol, diethylene glycol and acetaldehyde. This process has been modelled using Matlab and Simulink.

PROCEL

A first prototype of a fully automated pilot plant embedding continuous and batch operations was built on 1990 where planning activity, detailed production scheduling tasks and individual equipment unit’s control were for the first time integrated, so that the plant automatically could run from product recipe and demand specifications.

A new version (PROCEL) was built on 1997 which offers heat integration opportunities for batch processes (methodology developed on 1995), followed by the consideration of combined heat and power (1996). Combined heat and waste minimization constituted also a pioneering work on 1994, effects of uncertainty in batch production scheduling were examined (1995) and combined energy and water minimization methods were developed (1997). The study of the supervisory control layer and batch plant-wide integrated control that started on 1995 still continues today with a sophisticated and online automated abnormal management and fault diagnosis system (2008).

The incorporation of the above developments in the upper layer of supply chain decision-making can be seen as a natural extension and translation of the single site planning and scheduling methods developed to a higher degree of complexity. In this sense, the integrated batch control, implemented in PROCEL and demonstrated in several forums through internet, constitutes a solid successful technological background and supports the expected success of this proposal when attempting the novel Supply Chain dynamic tracking, at the business level, capable of revising planned activities, updating uncertain parameters and reacting to the incidents that may arise in the Supply Chain in low-level decisions to achieve the highest performance of the Supply Chain capacity.

Additionally the PROCEL plant will be adapted as test-bed of algorithm developments for the Supply Chain scenario. This project has a significant industrial orientation and aims also at providing solution to industrial problems, which involve complex manufacturing networks under varying conditions. Several industrial case studies will be brought up including Pilot Scenario (PROCEL).
